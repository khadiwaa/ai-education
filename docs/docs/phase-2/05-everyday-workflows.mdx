# Module 5: Everyday Workflows

*Phase 2 · ~60–75 minutes · Practical*

You have the toolkit. This module turns it into **repeatable patterns** for the work
that fills an actual week: reviewing documents, researching, analyzing data, writing,
and preparing for meetings.

Each pattern has the same skeleton — worth memorizing because it *is* the course:

> **Give the real material → say what you need and for whom → iterate → verify what
> matters → the judgment call stays yours.**

---

## Reviewing a document

The pattern for anything important that's about to go out the door — a contract, a
proposal, a policy, a sensitive email:

1. **Attach the actual document.** ([Module 1](/docs/phase-2/claude-essentials#attaching-files-and-images) —
   never a paraphrase.)
2. **Give the stakes and the lens:**

   ```text
   Attached is a proposal we're sending to a prospective client Friday.
   Review it as a skeptical reader on their side. I care most about:
   ambiguous commitments, inconsistencies (numbers, dates, names),
   anything missing a reader would expect, and tone problems.
   List issues by section, most serious first.
   ```

3. **Ask risk-oriented questions, not "is this good?"** — "What could be
   misread?", "Which claims lack support?", "Where do the numbers disagree?" A
   generic request gets a generic pat on the back; a named lens gets findings.
4. **Triage the findings yourself.** Claude will over-flag. You decide which issues
   are real — accepting every suggestion is *not* review, it's outsourcing judgment.

That last point is the heart of it: **Claude reviews the document; you review Claude.**
The sign-off — "this goes out under my name" — was never delegated.

## Research and summarization

Two research modes, and confusing them is how people get burned:

- **Explaining** — "Help me understand how X works." Low risk: you're building
  intuition, and errors surface as confusion.
- **Fact-finding** — "What are the current rules / prices / players?" Higher risk:
  the output looks identical whether it's right or
  [hallucinated](/docs/phase-1/using-ai-effectively#hallucination), and you're about
  to repeat it to someone.

The fact-finding pattern:

1. **Push the work onto real sources.** Have Claude search the web
   ([Module 4](/docs/phase-2/connectors-and-skills#what-connectors-do)) or attach the
   authoritative documents — ground the answer, don't test Claude's memory.
2. **Ask for sources inline** — "cite a source for each claim" — so every claim
   arrives with a way to check it.
3. **Make uncertainty visible:** "Separate what your sources establish from what
   you're inferring. Say 'I couldn't verify this' where that's true." Claude is
   good at this *when asked*.
4. **Verify what you'll rely on.** Names, numbers, dates, quotes, and legal or
   safety claims get checked against the actual source — click through; don't trust
   that a citation says what it's claimed to say.

### Quick search vs. deep research

For bigger questions, Claude (like most AI apps now) offers a **research mode**: instead
of one search, it works autonomously for several minutes — running dozens of searches,
reading sources, and assembling a long, cited report. It's the
[agentic loop](/docs/phase-1/how-ai-works#the-agentic-loop) at full stretch, and it
changes how you should use it:

- **Use it for questions that deserve an afternoon**, not a glance: "compare how our
  three main competitors price and package their services," "what should we know
  before expanding into [market]?" For a quick fact, regular search is faster and
  cheaper (research runs use much more of your usage budget).
- **Brief it like you'd brief a person doing a day of research** — the
  [first-message ingredients](/docs/phase-2/claude-essentials#writing-a-good-first-message)
  matter *more* here, because you can't steer mid-run. Say what decision the research
  feeds, what to include and exclude, and what a useful deliverable looks like.
- **The output is a strong first pass, not a finished analysis.** It arrives with
  citations — which get exactly the same click-through treatment as everything else.
  A 20-page cited report *feels* authoritative; that feeling is not verification.

For summarization, one upgrade: tell Claude **what the summary is for**. "Summarize
for someone deciding whether to attend" produces a different — and more useful —
document than "summarize this."

## Analyzing files and data

Claude handles spreadsheets and data files well — including doing real calculations,
not just talking about them. The pattern:

1. **Attach the actual file** — the spreadsheet itself, not a screenshot of it.
2. **Explain the data like you'd brief a temp:** what the rows are, what ambiguous
   columns mean, known quirks ("region codes changed in March; Q1 uses the old ones").
   Claude sees the values, not the tribal knowledge behind them.
3. **State the question precisely.** "Which customers' monthly totals declined for
   three consecutive months?" beats "any insights?" — vague questions produce
   plausible-sounding generic answers.
4. **Ask for the working, not just the answer:** "Show how you got this — which
   columns, what steps, what you excluded." For real computation, Claude can use its
   analysis tool to run actual code rather than estimating — for anything beyond
   trivial arithmetic, ask it to compute, not eyeball.
5. **Spot-check before acting.** Trace two or three results back to the raw rows.
   If they hold, trust rises; if not, you just saved yourself a bad decision.

:::warning The believability trap
A table of AI-produced numbers looks exactly as authoritative when it's subtly wrong.
Nothing derived from data goes into a decision or a deck until *some* of it has been
traced back to the source.
:::

## Writing and editing

Beyond the drafting loop from
[Module 3](/docs/phase-2/creating-with-artifacts#iterating-on-a-draft), the patterns
that earn daily use:

- **The hard email.** Declining, escalating, delivering bad news: give Claude the
  situation, the relationship, and the outcome you want — ask for two versions
  ("one warmer, one firmer") and blend. Cheaper than staring at a blank draft for
  40 minutes.
- **Tone translation.** "Rewrite this for the board / for the whole team / for a
  customer" — same facts, recalibrated register.
- **Editor mode, not writer mode.** For text you've written: "Tighten this without
  changing meaning or voice. List what you changed." The change-list keeps you in
  control of your own voice.
- **Unblocking.** Can't start? "Ask me five questions about what I'm trying to say,
  then draft from my answers." Talking beats staring.

## Meetings and planning

- **Prep:** attach the agenda and materials → "What should I read carefully vs.
  skim? What three questions should I be ready to answer? What's likely to be
  contentious?"
- **After:** paste or attach your rough notes (mind your organization's rules on
  recordings) → "Turn these into: decisions made, action items with owners, open
  questions." From [Module 2](/docs/phase-2/projects-and-instructions#project-instructions):
  if you do this weekly, it's a Project with the format in instructions — or, per
  [Module 4](/docs/phase-2/connectors-and-skills#what-skills-are), a skill.
- **Thinking partner:** before a decision — "Here's the situation and the options
  I see. Argue against my preferred option. What am I not considering?" Claude is
  excellent at generating the disagreement your team might be too polite to voice.
  The decision, as always, is yours.

## Making it stick: moving your work over, little by little

A quiet truth about everything above: **nobody adopts AI by redesigning their job.**
What actually happens — for everyone who ends up using it well — is that ordinary
work migrates over one task at a time, through trial and error, over months. That's
not a failure to do it properly; that *is* the method. So do it deliberately:

**1. Install the trigger question.** The hard part isn't prompting — it's
*remembering AI exists* while you're heads-down in the old way of doing something.
Build one reflex: whenever a task makes you sigh — the blank page, the third
reformatting, the report you're dreading — ask *"could Claude do a first pass of
this?"* The sigh is the trigger. You'll forget constantly at first; that's normal.
(A sticky note on your monitor with just "first pass?" is not a joke — it works.)

**2. Run cheap experiments on real work.** When the trigger fires, try it — on the
actual task, not a toy version. You already know the odds: some tries will save you
an hour, some will produce something you throw away. Both outcomes are the system
working. The experiment cost you two minutes; the win pays for twenty flops.

**3. When a try disappoints, diagnose before you conclude.** A mediocre result on the
first attempt is almost never "AI can't do this" — it's usually a one-line briefing
problem. Before writing the task off, check the
[usual suspects](/docs/phase-2/claude-essentials#writing-a-good-first-message): did it
have the goal, the real material (attached, not described), the audience, the format?
One retry with a better brief changes most verdicts.

**4. Keep score.** A running note — two columns, *worked* and *didn't* — turns
scattered experiments into a personal playbook. "Meeting notes → action items:
great. First-draft client emails: great. Final tone on sensitive emails: mine."
After a month you'll have something nobody can give you: a map of where AI helps
*your* specific job.

**5. Ratchet the winners.** When something works twice, stop treating it as an
experiment: give it a home. That's the progression this whole phase has been
building —

> in-the-moment try → repeat win → [Project](/docs/phase-2/projects-and-instructions#what-is-a-project)
> with the context baked in → eventually a [skill](/docs/phase-2/connectors-and-skills#what-skills-are)
> your whole team shares

Each step up the ratchet makes the win automatic instead of remembered.

**6. Expect the migration to be uneven — and let it be.** After six months, a typical
knowledge worker has moved maybe a third of their recurring work into some AI-assisted
form, kept the judgment-heavy core exactly as it was, and stopped thinking of any of
it as "using AI" — it's just how the report gets written now. That's what success
looks like. Not a transformation; a ratchet.

:::tip One deliberate rep per week
If the trigger habit is slow to form, schedule it: fifteen minutes, once a week, take
one task from your *didn't-try-yet* pile and run the experiment. Twelve weeks of that
beats any training course — including this one.
:::

## A day with Claude

What this looks like assembled — Tuesday, operations manager:

- **8:45** — Project *Weekly Update*: paste raw notes, get the draft in house format,
  two revision passes, human check on numbers, send.
- **10:00** — Vendor proposal review (the [document pattern](#reviewing-a-document)):
  attach, skeptical-reader lens, 11 findings, 4 real. Fix those.
- **11:30** — Prep for the pricing meeting: attach deck → three questions to be ready
  for; ask for the argument *against* the proposal; walk in prepared.
- **2:00** — Analyze the returns spreadsheet (the [data pattern](#analyzing-files-and-data)):
  brief the columns, precise question, ask for the working, spot-check three rows.
  One finding goes in the update — verified first.
- **4:30** — Two hard emails via two-versions-and-blend. Final reads: human.

Nothing exotic — the same five habits, on rotation, saving an hour here and thirty
minutes there. And note what this day really is: the *end state* of the ratchet above.
Six months ago, each of these was a sighed-at task and a first experiment.

## Vocabulary from this module

| Term | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Risk-oriented review** | Asking for specific failure modes (ambiguity, inconsistency, gaps) instead of "is this good?" |
| **Fact-finding vs. explaining** | Research modes with different risk: verify facts; explanations self-correct |
| **Spot-check** | Tracing a sample of AI-produced results back to the raw source before acting |
| **Editor mode** | Having Claude tighten your text with a change-list, preserving your voice |
| **Research mode** | An autonomous multi-search run producing a long cited report — for questions that deserve an afternoon |
| **The ratchet** | Promoting repeat wins: in-the-moment try → recurring habit → Project → shared skill |

## Key Takeaways

1. **One skeleton, every workflow:** real material in → clear ask → iterate →
   verify what matters → judgment stays human.
2. **Reviews need a lens.** Stakes plus named failure modes turn a pat on the back
   into findings — then you triage them.
3. **Research splits by risk.** Explanations are cheap to get wrong; facts aren't —
   ground in sources, get citations, click through.
4. **Data work is brief-compute-check.** Explain the data, ask precisely, demand the
   working, spot-check before anything reaches a decision.
5. **Adoption is a ratchet, not a leap.** Trigger question → cheap experiment on real
   work → diagnose flops before concluding → keep score → promote winners to Projects
   and skills. One task at a time is the method, not a compromise.
