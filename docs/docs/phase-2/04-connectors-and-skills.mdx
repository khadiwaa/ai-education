# Module 4: Connectors, MCP & Skills

*Phase 2 · ~45–60 minutes · Hands-on*

Out of the box, Claude knows what it learned in training plus whatever you put in the
chat. This module is about extending that in two directions:

- **Connectors** give Claude *reach* — access to live tools and data: your calendar,
  your documents, the web.
- **Skills** give Claude *expertise* — packaged instructions for doing a specific job
  your way, every time.

You'll finish this module by installing a real skill: this course's own self-assessment.

---

## What connectors do

A **connector** is a bridge between Claude and another tool you use — Google Drive,
your calendar, email, project trackers, and hundreds of others. Under the hood,
connectors are built on the **Model Context Protocol (MCP)** — an open standard
(created by Anthropic, adopted across the industry) for how AI apps talk to tools.
Think of MCP as the USB standard, and each connector as one cable.

Why you care — it changes the kind of question you can ask:

| Without connectors | With connectors |
|---|---|
| "Here's my schedule, pasted in — when am I free?" | "When am I free for a 90-minute meeting with the design team this week?" |
| Download report → attach → ask | "Pull the latest ops report from Drive and summarize what changed since last month" |
| "You said this stat was from 2024 — anything newer?" | Claude searches the web and cites current sources |

The shift, in Phase 1 terms: connectors are
[tools in the agentic loop](/docs/phase-1/how-ai-works#what-happens-when-you-send-a-prompt).
Claude stops being limited to its training data and your attachments, and starts
working with your *actual, current* information.

**Web search** is the connector-like capability you'll use most. When a question
involves anything after Claude's knowledge cutoff — prices, people, news, laws — make
sure it searches rather than recalls, and ask for sources. (More on verification in
[Phase 3](/docs/phase-3/verifying-ai-output).)

You'll find connectors in **Settings → Connectors**: browse the directory, connect
the tools you use, and each one typically asks you to sign in to that service and
approve what Claude may access.

## Before you enable a connector

Connectors are the moment Claude goes from "app I type into" to "app with access to my
stuff." That deserves 60 seconds of thought per connector — four questions:

1. **What can it read?** Your whole Drive or one folder? All email or one inbox?
   Prefer the narrowest access that does the job (the classic security principle of
   **least privilege**).
2. **What can it *do*?** Reading your calendar is one thing; sending email or editing
   documents is another. Be deliberately slower to grant *act* permissions than
   *read* permissions — remember
   [blast radius from Phase 1](/docs/phase-1/ai-in-your-workflow#human-in-the-loop).
3. **Who built it?** Official connectors from companies you already trust are one
   thing; a third-party connector from an unknown developer deserves the same
   skepticism as an unknown app asking for your files.
4. **Does your organization allow it?** Connecting work data to any tool is a policy
   question, not just a personal one. If there's an IT or security team, this is
   their lane — ask first.

:::warning Connected content is untrusted content
One more Phase 1 callback: once Claude reads external content — web pages, shared
documents, emails — it can encounter
[indirect prompt injection](/docs/phase-1/ai-in-your-workflow#prompt-injection-mandatory-security-knowledge):
text crafted to manipulate the AI reading it. This is a real reason to keep *act*
permissions narrow and approve consequential actions yourself, not a reason to avoid
connectors entirely.
:::

A sensible rollout for yourself, or a team: start with **one or two read-only
connectors that remove your most annoying copy-paste** (usually files + calendar), live
with them for a couple of weeks, then expand.

## What skills are

A **skill** is packaged know-how: a small bundle of instructions (and sometimes
reference files) that teaches Claude to do a specific job a specific way. Install one
once, and Claude automatically applies it whenever it's relevant — you just ask
normally.

The difference from a good prompt: a prompt is something *you* remember to write; a
skill is expertise that's *always loaded*. That makes skills the right tool when:

- the job **recurs** — weekly reports, intake checklists, standard reviews
- the job has **rules worth enforcing every time** — formats, rubrics, checklists
- **several people** should get the same quality — install the same skill, get the
  same process

Anthropic ships some skills built-in (e.g., producing polished Word/Excel/PowerPoint
files), and anyone can create one — including the team behind this course.

## Installing a skill

Time to do it for real — install this course's assessment skill:

1. **Download it:** [ai-assessment-skill.zip](/downloads/ai-assessment-skill.zip)
   (a small zip file — don't unzip it).
2. In the Claude app, open **Settings → Capabilities → Skills** and upload the zip.
   (Menus evolve; if you don't see it, search Settings for "skills." Skills require a
   paid plan.)
3. Open any chat and say:

```text
Assess me on phase 1 module 1
```

Claude will interview you — a few open-ended questions, one at a time — score your
answers against the course rubric, build you a personal review plan with links back
into this site, and end with a results block you paste into the
<a href="/assessments#import">Assessments page</a> to track your progress.

Notice what just happened, because it's the whole idea of skills in miniature: you
didn't paste a rubric, explain a scoring system, or describe a report format. You said
four words, and Claude knew the entire process. **That's what packaged expertise
feels like** — and anything your team does repeatedly could work the same way.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Connectors change the question you can ask** — from "here's my data, pasted" to
   "go look at my actual data."
2. **MCP is the standard underneath** — one open protocol, many connectors, and the
   same idea works across AI tools.
3. **Four checks before enabling:** what it reads, what it can do, who built it,
   what your organization's policy says. Read before act; narrow before broad.
4. **Skills are always-loaded expertise.** Repeated, rule-bound, multi-person jobs
   belong in a skill, not in everyone's memory of the right prompt.
5. **You've installed one.** The assessment skill is your working example of how
   packaged expertise feels in practice.
