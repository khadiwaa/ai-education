# Module 2: Projects, Memory & Custom Instructions

*Phase 2 · ~45–60 minutes · Hands-on*

Module 1 ended with a problem: every new chat starts from zero, so you find yourself
pasting the same background — who you are, what the client cares about, how your team
formats things — over and over.

This module is about making context **persistent**: Projects, project knowledge,
custom instructions, and personal preferences. Set these up once and every
conversation starts already briefed.

---

## What is a Project?

A **Project** is a workspace that groups related chats and gives all of them shared
context. It has three parts:

- **Chats** — conversations that live inside the Project.
- **Knowledge** — files and notes you upload once; every chat in the Project can use
  them.
- **Instructions** — standing guidance ("you are helping with X; always do Y") that
  applies to every chat in the Project.

The rule of thumb for when to create one:

> **One-off question → plain chat. Recurring work → Project.**

Good Project candidates from a typical work week:

| Project | Knowledge you'd upload | Instructions you'd set |
|---|---|---|
| *Vendor contracts* | Your standard terms, a glossary, two examples of past redlines | "Flag deviations from our standard terms. Be precise, cite section numbers." |
| *Weekly leadership update* | Last month's updates, the template, org chart | "Match the template. Terse, numbers first, no adjectives." |
| *Hiring — Operations role* | Job description, rubric, anonymized example notes | "Help screen candidates against the rubric. Never guess at facts not in the notes." |

Projects are also an organizational win even before the shared-context benefits: three
months from now, every conversation about the vendor contract is in one place.

:::info Availability
Projects are a paid-plan feature. On team plans, Projects can also be shared with
colleagues — one well-stocked Project can brief the whole team's chats.
:::

## Project knowledge

Knowledge is where you put the **stable reference material** — the things you'd hand a
new colleague on day one:

- Background documents (the plan, the contract, the policy)
- Templates and past examples of "what good looks like"
- Glossaries: your acronyms, product names, team names
- Notes you write directly ("Context: we're mid-negotiation with X; pricing discussion
  is off-limits until legal signs off")

Every chat inside the Project starts with this material available — you stop
re-attaching and re-explaining.

Two habits keep knowledge useful:

1. **Curate, don't dump.** Ten well-chosen documents beat a hundred stale ones. Knowledge
   competes for the model's attention like everything else in the
   [context window](/docs/phase-1/using-ai-effectively#the-context-window).
2. **Keep it current.** An out-of-date org chart in knowledge is worse than none — Claude
   will confidently use it. When a document changes, replace it.

### How Claude actually reads knowledge

A harness detail worth knowing, because it changes how you set Projects up: when a
Project's knowledge is small, the app can put **all of it** into the context window —
Claude has genuinely read everything. When knowledge grows past what fits, the app
switches to **searching** it instead — retrieving only the chunks that look relevant
to your question (that's [RAG from Phase 1](/docs/phase-1/using-ai-effectively#rag-retrieval-augmented-generation),
running inside your Project).

Retrieval is good, but it can miss — which is why Claude occasionally whiffs on
something that's *definitely* in the knowledge. Three habits that work with the
machinery instead of against it:

- **Curation again, now with a reason:** a lean knowledge base stays in
  everything-gets-read territory.
- **Name files descriptively** — `acme-msa-2026-signed.pdf` retrieves better than
  `final_v3 (2).pdf`.
- **Point, don't hope.** When a specific document matters, say so: *"Check the
  pricing addendum in the project knowledge"* beats assuming it was retrieved.

:::tip A note about "memory"
Depending on your plan, Claude may also offer a **memory** feature — remembering
useful details across conversations automatically. Worth understanding what that
really is: the model never "learns" you (weights are
[frozen](/docs/phase-1/how-ai-works#training-vs-inference)) — the app stores notes
and injects the relevant ones into future chats, just like everything else in the
context package.

Treat it as a bonus, not the plan: Projects with explicit knowledge are deliberate,
inspectable, and editable. And give memory an occasional privacy check-in — review
what's been remembered in Settings, and prune it; it's one of the quiet places
[sensitive details can accumulate](/docs/phase-3/confidentiality-and-privacy).
:::

## Project instructions

Knowledge is *what Claude should know*. Instructions are *how Claude should behave* —
standing guidance applied to every chat in the Project:

```text
You're helping the operations team draft and review vendor contracts.

- Always cite the section number when referring to the contract.
- When you flag a risk, rate it high/medium/low and say why.
- Our templates and standard terms are in the project knowledge —
  treat deviations from them as findings, not defaults.
- If information is missing, say so. Never fill gaps with assumptions.
```

Notice what makes these good instructions — the same ingredients as a good first
message from [Module 1](/docs/phase-2/claude-essentials#writing-a-good-first-message),
just made permanent: role, format rules, where to look, and what *not* to do.

Anything you've typed into three different chats belongs in the Project's instructions.

## Preferences vs project instructions

Instructions exist at more than one level, and knowing what goes where saves you from
repeating yourself — or from rules colliding.

| Layer | Where it's set | Applies to | Belongs there |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Personal preferences** | Settings → your profile | *Every* chat, everywhere | How you like to be answered, always: "Be concise. Use plain language. When I ask for options, give trade-offs." Plus stable facts: your role, your time zone. |
| **Project instructions** | Inside each Project | Chats in that Project | Context-specific rules: the client, the format, the tone for *this* work. |
| **The chat itself** | Your messages | This conversation | The task at hand, one-off adjustments: "shorter", "more formal", "audience is the board". |

The layers stack: preferences + project instructions + your message all shape each
answer, with the most specific layer winning when they conflict.

A quick test for placement: *"Would I want this in every single chat, forever?"* If
yes → personal preferences. *"In every chat about this work?"* → project
instructions. *"Just right now?"* → say it in the chat.

**Styles** are a related shortcut: reusable tone presets (concise, explanatory,
formal…) you can switch per conversation without rewriting instructions. If you keep
typing "make it more formal," make it a style instead.

## Try it now

Build one real Project, end to end (~20 minutes):

1. Pick the most repetitive AI-suited work you have — the thing you've already
   explained to Claude more than once.
2. Create a Project. Upload 3–5 genuinely useful documents to knowledge — including
   at least one *example of a good past output*, which is worth more than any
   description of what you want.
3. Write instructions using the template above: role, format rules, where to look,
   what not to do.
4. Start a chat inside it and do the actual task. Note how much shorter your message
   is now that the briefing already happened.
5. Separately: open Settings and write two sentences of personal preferences you want
   in *every* chat.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Recurring work deserves a Project.** One-off questions don't.
2. **Knowledge = what Claude should know.** Stable, curated, current reference
   material — including examples of good output.
3. **Instructions = how Claude should behave.** Anything you've typed three times
   belongs there.
4. **Layer your guidance.** Everywhere-always → preferences; this-work-always →
   project instructions; just-now → the chat.
5. **Explicit beats automatic.** Memory features are convenient, but curated Projects
   are the system you control.
