# Module 5: Staying Current

*Phase 3 · ~30 minutes · Ongoing*

AI is the fastest-moving technology of your working life, and it's covered by the
loudest media environment in history. The combination produces a specific anxiety:
*I can't keep up, everyone else is ahead, and everything I learned is already stale.*

None of that is true. This module is a sustainable information diet — what to watch,
what to ignore, and how to stay comfortably current in about an hour a month.

---

## What changes fast vs. what doesn't

The secret to low-anxiety currency is knowing which layer you're looking at:

| Layer | Pace | Examples |
|-------|------|----------|
| **Model names & benchmarks** | Weeks | This month's leaderboard, version numbers |
| **Product features** | Months | New connector, better file handling, longer context |
| **Your skills** | Years | Everything in this curriculum |

Here's the reassuring part: **this curriculum was deliberately built on the slow
layer.** Briefing well, grounding in sources, iterating on drafts, the traffic-light
rule, stakes-based verification — none of that expires when a new model ships. New
models make your existing skills *more* powerful, not obsolete.

So the goal isn't following the news. It's noticing the handful of changes per year
that actually alter what you should *do*.

---

## The only three questions that matter

When a new model or feature launches, skip the hype and ask:

1. **Does it change what's possible for my work?** (New capability — e.g., it can now
   process the kind of files or tasks it couldn't before.)
2. **Does it change what's safe?** (New data terms, new settings, new policy
   implications — reread [Module 1](/docs/phase-3/confidentiality-and-privacy).)
3. **Does it change what things cost?** (Plan changes worth a yearly re-look —
   [Module 3](/docs/phase-3/choosing-tools-and-plans).)

If a launch clears none of the three, it's entertainment. Most launches are
entertainment.

---

## A sustainable information diet

**Daily: nothing.** Genuinely. Daily AI news is noise by construction.

**Monthly (~30 minutes):** one skim of what shipped from the major providers — the
announcement pages of the tools you actually use (for Claude:
[anthropic.com/news](https://www.anthropic.com/news)) beat any commentator. Run the
three questions. Usually the answer is "nothing to do."

**Quarterly (~30 minutes):** one deliberate experiment. Pick a task you do regularly,
try it with a feature or approach you haven't used — a connector you never enabled, a
skill, extended thinking on a hard problem. Real experiments teach more than a year
of reading. (This is the same muscle as
[Phase 2's ratchet](/docs/phase-2/everyday-workflows#making-it-stick-moving-your-work-over-little-by-little)
— pointed at new capabilities instead of new tasks.)

**Yearly (~1 hour):** the reset. Re-run [Module 3's five real tasks](/docs/phase-3/choosing-tools-and-plans#the-evaluation-checklist)
comparison, reread your organization's policy against current reality, and prune —
what did you set up that you never use?

**What to ignore on purpose:** benchmark horse-races, "X is dead" takes, AGI-timeline
discourse, demo videos of products you can't buy yet, and anyone who is certain about
what happens in five years. All heat, no decisions.

:::tip Let Claude do the monitoring
A pleasing loop: use the tool to stay current on the tool. Monthly prompt —
*"Search for what shipped in [your AI tools] in the last month. For someone using it
for [your kind of work], what — if anything — changes what I should do?"* Ground it
with search, and apply [Module 2's](/docs/phase-3/verifying-ai-output) skepticism to
the answer.
:::

## Evaluating a new tool or model in 30 minutes

When something does clear the three questions — or your team asks "should we switch?"
— don't read reviews. Run your own eval:

1. Take the same **five real tasks** from your [Module 3 pilot](/docs/phase-3/choosing-tools-and-plans#a-sane-buying-process)
   (keep them written down; they're your permanent yardstick).
2. Run them head-to-head against your current setup.
3. Score: meaningfully better, same, or worse — *on your work*, which is the only
   benchmark that matters.
4. Switch only for decisive wins ([Module 3's](/docs/phase-3/choosing-tools-and-plans#thinking-about-cost-like-an-owner)
   switching costs are real).

## For teams: making currency a ritual

If you run the [sharing loop from Module 4](/docs/phase-3/bringing-ai-to-your-team#the-rollout-in-order),
staying current is already 80% handled — new discoveries surface at the monthly demo.
Add:

- **A named watcher.** One person (a champion, rotating is fine) owns the monthly
  skim and posts a three-line summary: *what shipped / what it means for us / what,
  if anything, we're changing.* Everyone else is thereby licensed to ignore the news.
- **A yearly curriculum touch-up.** If you forked this course, once a year swap stale
  examples and note what changed. The
  [contributing guide](https://github.com/alexkhadiwala-hl/ai-education/blob/main/CONTRIBUTING.md)
  is set up for exactly this.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Skills are the slow layer.** Models churn weekly; everything this course taught
   you compounds for years.
2. **Three questions filter everything:** does it change what's possible, what's safe,
   or what it costs? No → entertainment.
3. **The diet: monthly skim, quarterly experiment, yearly reset.** About an hour a
   month, total.
4. **Your five tasks are your benchmark.** Evaluate every shiny thing against your
   own work, nothing else.
5. **Delegate the watching.** One named person (or Claude itself) monitors; everyone
   else gets to work.
