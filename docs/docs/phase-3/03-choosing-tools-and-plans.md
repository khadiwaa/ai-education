# Module 3: Choosing AI Tools & Plans

*Phase 3 · ~40 minutes · Strategic*

At some point the question shifts from "how do I use this?" to "what should we buy?"
— for yourself, and eventually for a team. This module gives you a durable way to
answer it. Deliberately no price tables or feature matrices: those go stale in
months. The *questions* don't.

---

## The landscape in one paragraph

A handful of frontier providers (Anthropic, OpenAI, Google, and a changing cast of
others) build the top models; everyone else builds on top of them. Capabilities
leapfrog every few months, which means two things: **today's "best model" ranking is a
snapshot, not a decision**, and switching costs matter more than launch-day
benchmarks. Choose on the durable axes below, not on this quarter's leaderboard.

---

## Plan tiers: what you're actually buying

Almost every AI product ladders the same way. What changes at each rung:

| Tier | What it typically adds | Who it's for |
|------|------------------------|--------------|
| **Free** | The product, with usage caps and fewer features | Evaluating; occasional use |
| **Individual paid** (~$20–30/seat/month) | Higher limits, better models, the power features — Projects, skills, connectors | Anyone using AI for real work weekly |
| **Team** (per-seat, slightly higher) | Central billing and admin, shared workspaces/Projects, and — critically — **business data terms** (typically including no-training commitments) | Any group using AI on internal material |
| **Enterprise** | SSO, audit and compliance controls, contractual data-processing terms, admin governance | Organizations with compliance obligations or scale |

Two non-obvious notes:

- **The jump that matters most is usually free → paid** for capability, and
  **individual → team for data terms.** If staff are doing real work on personal
  free accounts, you have both a capability gap and a
  [confidentiality gap](/docs/phase-3/confidentiality-and-privacy#the-training-question).
- **"API access" is a different product** — pay-per-use building blocks for software
  developers, not something a knowledge-work team needs to think about. If a vendor
  quotes you per-token prices, you're in the wrong meeting.

## The evaluation checklist

When comparing tools (or re-evaluating the one you have), score on five axes:

**1. Data handling** *(the gating question — see [Module 1](/docs/phase-3/confidentiality-and-privacy))*
Training commitments on your tier? Retention and deletion? Admin visibility?
Certifications your industry needs? If this fails, nothing else matters.

**2. Capability on *your* work**
Not benchmarks — your actual tasks. Take five real ones (the proposal, the analysis,
the review) and run them head-to-head. An hour of this beats every published
comparison, because the ranking depends on the work.

**3. The workflow features**
Phase 2's leverage came from features, not raw model IQ: Projects, attachments,
artifacts, connectors, skills. Does the candidate have equivalents? How good are they
in practice?

**4. Team fit**
Admin controls, shared workspaces, provisioning, and — often decisive — what your
organization *already* pays for (bundled AI in your existing office suite may be
"good enough" for some users, even if a dedicated tool is better).

**5. Trajectory and lock-in**
Is the provider shipping? Are your Projects/data exportable if you leave? Prefer
tools built on open standards ([MCP](/docs/phase-2/connectors-and-skills#what-connectors-do)
for integrations) — your setup investment survives a switch.

## Thinking about cost like an owner

The arithmetic that matters for a business:

```text
Seat cost:        ~$25–60/person/month  →  ~$300–700/year
Break-even:       at $50/hour loaded cost, that's 6–14 hours/year
                  → about 30–70 minutes saved per month
```

Phase 2 users don't save 30 minutes a month; they save hours a week. For anyone who
writes, researches, or analyzes for a living, seat price is a rounding error —
**the real costs are elsewhere:**

- **Training time** — the gap between "has a license" and "uses it well" is exactly
  the gap this curriculum exists to close. Unused seats are the most common AI waste.
- **Rollout attention** — someone has to own policy, onboarding, and questions
  ([Module 4](/docs/phase-3/bringing-ai-to-your-team)).
- **Verification debt** — unverified AI output that ships is a cost you pay later,
  with interest ([Module 2](/docs/phase-3/verifying-ai-output)).

So the honest ROI question is rarely "can we afford the seats?" — it's "will we do
the adoption work that makes the seats pay?"

## A sane buying process

For a small team, this is a two-week decision, not a procurement odyssey:

1. **Shortlist two or three** candidates that pass the data-handling gate.
2. **Pilot with 3–5 real users on real work** for two weeks — include a skeptic, not
   just enthusiasts.
3. **Compare notes against the five axes.** Capability differences will be smaller
   than expected; workflow and data-terms differences bigger.
4. **Buy the team tier, write the one-page policy** ([Module 1](/docs/phase-3/confidentiality-and-privacy#if-your-organization-has-no-policy-yet)),
   and roll out with training ([Module 4](/docs/phase-3/bringing-ai-to-your-team)).
5. **Re-evaluate yearly, lightly.** The landscape shifts fast, but switching has real
   costs — re-run the five real tasks once a year and switch only for a decisive gap.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Choose on durable axes, not leaderboards.** Data handling, capability on your
   work, workflow features, team fit, lock-in.
2. **Data terms gate everything.** The individual→team jump is usually about
   commitments, not features.
3. **Seats are cheap; adoption is the real cost.** Budget for training and ownership,
   not just licenses.
4. **Pilot on your own work.** Five real tasks, two weeks, include a skeptic.
5. **Re-evaluate annually, switch rarely.** Prefer open standards so your investment
   ports.
