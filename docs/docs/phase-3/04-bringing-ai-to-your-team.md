# Module 4: Bringing AI to Your Team

*Phase 3 · ~45 minutes · Strategic*

You've built personal AI fluency. If you lead people — a team, a department, a company
— the next multiplier isn't you getting better; it's everyone else getting to where
you are. This module is the playbook.

---

## What actually happens without a plan

Buy licenses, send the announcement, hope. Six months later, every team that does
this finds the same distribution:

- **~15% enthusiasts** found it themselves, use it daily, quality varies wildly
- **~60% tried it twice** — got a mediocre result from a one-line prompt (a
  [Phase 2, Module 1](/docs/phase-2/claude-essentials#writing-a-good-first-message)
  failure, not a tool failure) and quietly went back to the old way
- **~25% never logged in** — some skeptical, some afraid, some just busy

Meanwhile the enthusiasts have invented their own rules about what's safe to paste,
which is how confidentiality incidents happen. **Adoption without education produces
exactly the two failure modes that matter: unused seats and unsafe use.**

---

## The three fears (address them or nothing else lands)

Resistance to AI at work is rarely about the tool. It's about three unspoken fears —
and each has an honest answer:

1. **"This will take my job."** The honest version: the job *changes* — the tedious
   drafting shrinks, and judgment, relationships, and expertise become a larger share
   of the work. People who use AI well become more valuable, not less. Say this
   explicitly, and mean it — if leadership actually views AI as a headcount tool,
   no training program will fix the trust problem.
2. **"I'll look stupid."** Fear of asking basic questions in public kills adoption
   quietly. The fixes: normalize being a beginner (leaders sharing their own clumsy
   first prompts works wonders), and make help low-stakes (a chat channel beats a
   scheduled clinic).
3. **"I'll break a rule I don't know about."** Absent a clear policy, careful people
   abstain and careless people improvise — the worst of both. The one-page policy
   from [Module 1](/docs/phase-3/confidentiality-and-privacy#if-your-organization-has-no-policy-yet)
   is the antidote, and it must lead with *what's allowed*, not just what's banned.

---

## The rollout, in order

The sequence matters — each step de-risks the next:

**1. Policy before licenses.** One page: approved tools, traffic-light rules, who to
ask, what to do about mistakes. Without it, training teaches people to do unsafe
things faster.

**2. Pilot with a mixed handful.** 3–5 people, *including a skeptic*, on real work for
two weeks ([Module 3](/docs/phase-3/choosing-tools-and-plans#a-sane-buying-process)).
Their real examples become your training material — "here's how Dana cut the monthly
report from four hours to one" beats any vendor demo.

**3. Train on *their* work, not on AI.** People don't want to learn AI; they want
their annoying tasks to hurt less. Structure everything around before/after demos of
tasks your team actually does. (This curriculum is built for exactly this — Phase 1
for shared mental models, Phase 2 for hands-on skill. Fork it, swap in your examples,
assign it.)

**4. Name champions.** One person per team who's visibly good, explicitly allowed to
spend a couple of hours a week helping others, and connected to the other champions.
Champions surface what training misses: the team-specific workflows, the confusion
patterns, the policy edge cases.

**5. Build the sharing loop.** A monthly 30 minutes — three people show one thing they
did with AI ("show your prompts, show the flops too"), plus a running channel for
wins and near-misses ([Module 2's norm](/docs/phase-3/verifying-ai-output#building-the-habit-into-a-team):
celebrate the catch). This does more than any second training session.

### A 30-60-90 sketch

| Days | Focus | Done when |
|------|-------|-----------|
| 0–30 | Policy, tool decision, pilot group working | Policy published; pilot has real before/afters |
| 30–60 | Everyone onboarded, curriculum assigned, champions named | Every seat activated; each team has a champion |
| 60–90 | Sharing loop running, first workflow wins captured | Monthly demo happened twice; 3+ documented team workflows (as Projects or [skills](/docs/phase-2/connectors-and-skills#what-skills-are)) |

## Measuring whether it's working

Don't measure prompts-per-day. Measure:

- **Activation** — % of seats used weekly (the honest adoption number)
- **Capability** — self-assessment scores from this curriculum, before and after
  (the <a href="/assessments#setup">assessment skill</a> gives you exactly this, per module)
- **Workflow capture** — count of team workflows turned into shared Projects or
  skills; these are adoption that *compounds*
- **Time reclaimed** — ask quarterly, one question: "which recurring task got
  meaningfully faster, and by roughly how much?" Self-reported and imprecise — and
  still the number leadership actually needs
- **Incidents** — confidentiality near-misses and verification catches. You *want*
  a nonzero report rate; zero means people aren't telling you

### When adoption stalls

It will, around month two. The usual causes, in order of likelihood: the training was
about AI instead of about their tasks; middle managers were skipped (train them
*first* — nothing dies faster than a practice your manager visibly ignores); the
policy is so cautious that safe uses feel risky; or the enthusiasts moved on. The
fix is almost always the sharing loop plus champions, not another all-hands.

---

## Key Takeaways

1. **Licenses ≠ adoption.** Without education you get unused seats and unsafe use —
   the two expensive failures.
2. **Answer the three fears out loud.** Job change, looking stupid, unknown rules.
   Honesty and a one-page policy handle most of it.
3. **Sequence: policy → pilot → task-based training → champions → sharing loop.**
   Each step de-risks the next.
4. **Train on their tasks, not on AI.** Before/after demos of real work convert
   skeptics; feature tours don't.
5. **Measure activation, capability, captured workflows, and reclaimed time** — and
   treat reported near-misses as health, not failure.
